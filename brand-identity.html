Used for editorial headlines, pull quotes, campaign slogans, and anywhere the human voice needs to come through. Italic weight preferred. Creates warmth and historical resonance — it carries the suffragette era without being pastiche.

06Textures

Print grain Digital backgrounds — social, web, decks
Rough paper Letterheads, zines, printed materials
Risograph Posters, flyers, event print runs
Newsprint Newsletters, campaign broadsheets
Woodcut grain Placards, protest materials, stickers
Dark screen Video backgrounds, projection, digital covers

How to use texture

  • Grain is a layer, not a style. Apply it as an overlay above your base colour — never replace flat colour with texture alone.
  • Keep it subtle. Texture should be felt, not read. If you can describe the texture more than the content, it is too heavy.
  • Match texture weight to material. Risograph for short runs under 500 copies. Newsprint for newsletters and broadsheets. Grain for digital. Woodcut for anything that gets carried or posted.
  • The mark always sits above texture. Never place the mark inside a textured field without testing legibility at A4 and A6.
  • Parchment + grain = the chapter default. When in doubt, this is the safe combination for any printed chapter material.

What texture says

  • This is not corporate. The roughness is intentional — it signals hand-made, grassroots, real.
  • Texture connects the digital and the printed. A social post and a flyposted bill should feel like they came from the same hand.
  • The woodcut register of the mark demands texture around it. A clean vector logo on a smooth white background reads as brand. The same mark on rough parchment reads as movement.
  • Texture is especially important for chapters producing materials locally. It allows lo-fi printing — photocopied or home-printed — to still feel coherent with the wider identity.

07Photography

Intersectional Uprising photography shows real people doing real things. It is not stock. It is not polished. It does not flatten the people in it. Every image should make someone feel seen — not represented.

01

The crowd

Wide shots of marches, streets, and gatherings. Shoot into the movement — from within, not above. The camera is at shoulder height or lower. Never aerial unless showing scale of a major march. The crowd is the subject. Individual faces may or may not be identifiable.

Eg: A street from pavement level, signs above heads, bodies in motion. Overcast light preferred.
02

The person

Portraits shot with consent and intention. Eye contact with lens or away — both work. No posed smiles. Expression should be open, direct, or contemplative. Crop tight to face or show full body and environment. Never mid-body crop that removes context.

Eg: A woman in her fifties, sign in hand, looking directly at lens. Available light. No flash.
03

The hand

Close detail of hands — holding placards, gripping banners, writing, painted, raised. Hands are one of the most honest subjects. They show labour, age, solidarity. Shoot at close range. Strong graphic contrast. Works especially well in near-monochrome.

Eg: Two hands different skin tones gripping the same placard pole. From below, sky behind.
04

The object

Placards, banners, badges, zines, stitched patches, painted shoes. Objects tell the story of effort — someone made this. Shoot on texture when possible: concrete, tarmac, rough wood. Never on white seamless. The object should carry evidence of use.

Eg: A placard face-up on wet pavement after a march. Lettering slightly smudged. Still readable.
05

The space

Streets before and after. Meeting rooms. Stairwells with flyposters. Chapter locations. The architecture of where organising happens. Wide, quiet, observational. No people necessary. These images give chapters a sense of place and presence.

Eg: A town hall exterior, one IU poster visible in a window. Late afternoon light.
06

The text

Words on walls, on windows, on skin. Graffiti, stencils, handwritten signs. Legible text as image. Shoot straight-on or at slight angle with strong depth. Works in colour or near-monochrome. The typographic energy of the movement, documented.

Eg: "UPRISING" chalked on a pavement. Shot from directly above. Pink chalk on grey stone.

Colour treatment

  • Shoot in colour — the palette does the editorial work in post
  • Push contrast. Shadows should be deep. Highlights clean. No flat, milky tones.
  • Violet and green grade well from available light. A slight cool shadow pull brings images into the palette naturally.
  • Duotone using Violet + Parchment for editorial use — chapter newsletters, annual reports, campaign headers.
  • Near-monochrome with a single colour accent — works well for social posts. Desaturate most of the image, keep one element in Uprising Red.
  • Full colour for march coverage — the energy and diversity of colour in a crowd is part of the message.

What to avoid

  • Stock photography. No exceptions. It reads as institution, not movement.
  • Bright flash or ring light. Hard frontal light flattens expression and signals commercial content.
  • Heavy Instagram-style filters — warm golden tones, heavy vignette, faded shadows. These read as lifestyle brand.
  • Posed group shots where everyone faces camera and smiles. Document, don't direct.
  • Cropping out disability aids, mobility aids, or assistive devices. They are part of the person.
  • Publishing photographs of individuals without consent — especially at protests. If identifiable, confirm before posting.
  • Centring whiteness in a collection or page spread. Review image selections at the edit stage.

08Chapter usage

You can

  • Use the mark in single colour — Almost Black on Parchment, or inverted on any brand colour.
  • Add your chapter city beneath the wordmark in DM Sans Light, tracked wide. Example: Intersectional Uprising — Sheffield
  • Print flyers, zines, and placards using any combination of the six brand colours.
  • Resize the mark at any scale as long as it remains legible. Minimum print size: 15mm tall. Minimum digital: 32px tall.
  • Apply texture overlays from the approved texture set to any printed material.
  • Use the full wordmark lockup or the mark alone — both are approved for chapter use.
  • Set local event copy in Bebas Neue and Cormorant Garamond. DM Sans for all body copy.
  • Reproduce in single colour black, or single colour white, for photocopied or screenprinted materials.

You cannot

  • Stretch, rotate, or distort the mark in any direction.
  • Place the mark on a photographic background without a clear colour block behind it.
  • Recolour the mark in any colour outside the six brand colours.
  • Add drop shadows, glows, gradients, or 3D effects to the mark.
  • Create a chapter sub-logo using a different graphic or icon alongside the mark.
  • Use the name Intersectional Uprising in a font other than Cormorant Garamond or Bebas Neue in any branded context.
  • Reproduce the mark at a size where the flame tail loses definition. If it blurs, scale up.
  • Alter the mark in any way — including removing the flame tail, simplifying paths, or adding elements. Contact national for an approved alternate if a specific need arises.
